Translational Synthetic Biology lab

Sept 2019 - Present, Pompeu Fabra University (Spain)

To strengthen my knowledge on synthetic biology and medicine, and complement it with computational approaches, I am actually studying for the MSc in Bioinformatics and Biostatistics of the University of Barcelona. For my master's thesis, I am working full time in the laboratory of Prof. Marc Güell, who was a postdoc at Harvard's genetics professor George Church's laboratory before starting his own lab in the Pompeu Fabra University. There, I am applying recent developments in the field of synthetic biology to solve health problems such as muscular distrophy or the need for new antibiotics. The projects I am involved with are:

  • Using machine learning to predict the efficiency of CRISPR-Cas9 genome engineering in vivo. By studying the mutations introduced by CRISPR-Cas9, prime editing or base editors at multiple sites of the genome, we are building a machine learning model to estimate the outcomes of genome engineering with each method. Hence, I am learning to use the most advanced genome engineering tools, to create adeno-associated viruses (AAVs) for their delivery, and to analyze sequencing data and interpret it to create a computational model.
  • Cell-free biosynthetic pathway engineering for the identification on novel antibiotics. We have developed a cell-free system with the metabolic pathways needed for the synthesis of an antimicrobial compound. We are using Multiplex Automated Genome Engineering (MAGE) to generate variants in the enzymes involved in this metabolic route with the objective of producing novel compounds with unique antimicrobial activities.
  • Increasing lentiviral payload capacity to use them as gene therapy for muscular dystrophy. Introducing variants by MAGE in lentiviral vectors we are increasing the size of the cassettes this vectors can carry. This will allow the delivery of larger transgenes, such as LAMA2 which could cure myotonic dystrophy type 2.

Gehrke lab, MIT

Sept. 2018 - July 2019, Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MA, USA)

For my thesis of the MSc in Bioengineering, I worked under the supervision of Prof. Lee Gehrke, reducing the development time and cost of paper-based diagnostics for emerging viruses like Dengue or Zika. Current production methods rely on the hybridoma technique for the identification of monoclonal antibodies binding to viral proteins. This can take up to 8 months and requires animal immunization, limiting the applicability of the tests for emerging viruses that could spread at any time. My knowledge in genetic engineering allowed us to use a yeast surface display library (created by Prof. Kruse and colleagues at Harvard Medical School) to identify nanobodies and develop tests for new pathogens in about one month. In addition, I improved the current yeast selection method by adding a quantitative step and characterized every step of the process so that it could be implemented elsewhere. I also designed and implemented a nanobody cloning method to produce them as recombinant proteins in E. coli or HEK293F cells. To culminate the project, I presented a poster in the Harvard Microbiology Department Retreat, where I had the chance to discuss the results with faculty and help other people implement the nanobody selection process in their labs. The title of the master thesis is: “Rapid identification of Nanobody Reagents for Emerging Viruses using Yeast Surface Display”. It was awarded with the Salvador Gil Prize to the best thesis of the MSc in Bioengineering of the Ramon Llull University.

BioIQS iGEM team

Feb. 2018 - Sept. 2018, IQS School of Engineering (Ramon Llull University, Spain)

I created a team to compete in the 2018 iGEM competition with the objective of experiencing how to have an impact in society through synthetic biology. From February 2018 to October 2018, I led a team of 12 master’s students to develop a personalized gluten sensor for celiac patients. Our sensor was based on the patient-specific variant of the HLA-DQ protein, which recognizes gluten epitopes and determines the sensibility of each patient. To build it, we isolated by PCR the hla-dq gene from saliva samples and expressed the regions involved in gluten detection. I worked on the design of the genetic system to produce recombinant HLA-DQ, which was challenging because the receptor consists of two polypeptide chains. The simple method we created not only facilitates the construction of the sensor but could also be used to study the interaction between HLA-DQ and gluten peptides at the structural level. This project taught me soft skills such as leadership, communication and team management. We collected 7,150.00€ ($7,950.00) from investors to develop our project and presented it at the 2018 Giant Jamboree, in front of more than 300 iGEM teams from all around the world. Our project was awarded the bronze medal of the competition.

Chemical and Environmental Engineering laboratory, LEQUIA

June 2016 - Sept. 2017, University of Girona (Spain)

For my senior, undergraduate thesis, I characterized the performance of a natural microbial isolate in a bioelectrochemical reactor. The reactor was fed with waste CO2 and electricity from renewable energy sources and the microorganism used them to produce ethanol. Since, initially, ethanol production was low, I carefully studied all the factors that could be influencing its production, read literature at the intersection of bioreactors engineering, metabolism, and thermodynamics, and autonomously interpreted experimental data to propose technical improvements. Specifically, my contributions changed the focus of the project from the identification of the ideal bioreactor conditions to the optimization of metabolic pathways. With further research, this approach led the group to increase ethanol production. The title of the bachelor thesis I completed at LEQUIA is “Ethanol production from carbon dioxide by an enriched culture of the isolate I19 using microbial electrochemical technologies”. The thesis was qualified with excellent and received a prize to the best undergraduate thesis on carbon capture technologies in Spain by the PTECO2 foundation.
